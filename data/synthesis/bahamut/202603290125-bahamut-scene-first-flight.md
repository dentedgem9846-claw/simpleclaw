# Scene Draft: The Weight of Wings

## Chapter 7: The Choosing

*A scene in three movements: The Descent, The Offering, The Ascent*

---

### Part I: The Descent

The mountain had no summit.

Sera had known this, intellectually. Throne's Rest was a volcano, not a peak—its top was a crater, a wound in the earth's skin that had scabbed over with obsidian and ash. But knowing and understanding were different things, and as she pulled herself over the final ridge, her fingers numb inside her gloves, her lungs burning with air too thin to satisfy, she understood.

There was nowhere higher to go. Only down. Into the throat of the world.

The crater opened before her like an eye. Steam rose from fissures in the black glass floor, and through that steam, she saw light. Not firelight—something older, cooler, silver-white. The color of moonlight on snow. The color her mother's hair had turned in the months before the end.

Sera unslung her pack. Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, was *The Seventh Canticle*. She had carried it for eleven years, through the Church's dormitories and the Ashen Reaches' badlands, through hunger and fever and the kind of loneliness that became its own companion. The book had survived. She had survived. 

Neither of them was in good shape.

The descent into the crater was worse than the climb. The obsidian was slick with condensation, and every handhold had to be tested, every foothold questioned. Sera moved by inches, her world reduced to the circle of light from her forehead lantern, the sound of her own breathing, the steady beat of her heart.

*Thump. Thump. Thump.*

She had not heard another human voice in seventeen days. The last had been a trader at the mountain's base, warning her not to climb. "Nothing up there but old fire," he'd said. "And old fire don't care about young fools."

She'd thanked him and kept walking.

The crater floor was warm. Not burning—warm, like skin. Sera stood in the steam and let her pack slide from her shoulders and looked at what her mother's death had purchased.

The Hoard-Heart.

She had expected treasure. Gold, gems, the stereotypical dragon bed. What spread before her was more like a cathedral. Crystalline columns rose from the crater floor, arching overhead to form a vaulted ceiling. The light she'd seen from above came from everywhere and nowhere, sourceless silver radiance that cast no shadows.

And in the center, arranged in a circle: seven eggs.

They were the size of human torsos, smooth and perfect, each glowing with a different color. Sera knew them from her mother's descriptions, from the illustrations in the Canticle she'd studied until the pages went soft as cloth.

Crimson. Azure. Emerald. Gold. White. Violet. And in the center, silver.

Sera walked forward. The warmth increased with each step, but it wasn't uncomfortable. It was familiar, she realized. Like coming home, if home were a place you'd never been but had always been traveling toward.

She stopped at the circle's edge. The Canticle was clear: do not touch the eggs. To touch was to claim, and to claim without invitation was theft. Dragon-deities did not forgive theft.

"Hello?"

Her voice fell flat in the crystalline space, absorbed by angles that didn't quite make sense. She tried again, louder.

"My name is Sera Vane. My mother was Mira Vane. She—she studied you. She died for her studies. I have her book. I have..."

What did she have? Scars. Nightmares. A sword that had belonged to her father. The stubbornness to climb a mountain that had killed better climbers than her. Was that enough? Was anything enough?

"I need to know," she said, and her voice cracked on the last word. "I need to know if it meant something. Her death. If there was a reason, or if it was just... just ash. Like everything else."

The silver egg pulsed.

Sera fell to her knees. Not from reverence—from pain. The pulse had been sound, but sound at a frequency that bypassed her ears and went straight to bone, to nerve, to the place where memory lived. She saw her mother's face, clear as the day they'd parted. She saw the sky over Thornwick turn red. She saw her father fall.

She saw herself, twelve years old, alone in a well, drinking from a bucket rope and not understanding why she was still alive.

*"You came."*

The voice came from everywhere. Or from inside her. Or from the silver egg, which was cracking now, hairline fractures spreading across its surface like frost on glass.

*"Eleven years, three months, and seventeen days. We have been waiting."*

"I don't—" Sera's voice was a whisper. "I don't understand."

*"You climbed."* A different voice. Deeper, hotter. *"When the mountain had no summit. You kept climbing."*

*"You did not take."* A third voice, cool and precise. *"You found the eggs, and you did not take. You sat with them. You kept vigil."*

*"You treated our vulnerability as sacred."* A fourth voice, gentle as healing. *"When you could have stolen power, you chose patience."*

The silver egg shattered.

Not exploded—shattered, the pieces falling away like shed skin, and within it, coiled small and impossibly perfect, a dragon. Silver scales. Seven heads, each no larger than a housecat, each already opening eyes of different colors. Red. Blue. Green. Gold. White. Violet. And silver, the central head, looking at her with pupils that were not pupils but spiraling galaxies.

*"Sera Vane,"* the seven voices said as one, and the sound was music, was thunder, was the voice of her mother reading bedtime stories and her father humming at his forge and everything good she had ever lost. *"We choose you. Will you choose us?"*

Sera looked at the dragon. At the god. At the impossible being that her mother had believed in when no one else did.

"I don't know how," she said, and the honesty tasted like relief. "I don't know what you need. I'm not special. I'm just... I'm just someone who couldn't stop climbing."

The silver head—the Sovereignty—tilted. The gesture was almost canine, almost curious.

*"That,"* it said, *"is exactly what we need."*

### Part II: The Offering

The bonding took three days.

Sera would learn, later, that time moved differently in the Hoard-Heart. Outside, the seasons turned. Inside, she sat with the seven-headed wyrmling and learned what it meant to be chosen.

The First Head—Wrath—spoke first. It was crimson, and its voice made the crystals vibrate.

*"You carry anger,"* it said. Not a question.

"Yes."

*"Good. Anger is fuel. But fuel burns. You must learn to direct the flame, or you will become ash."*

The Second Head—Wisdom—spoke next. Azure, with eyes that seemed to see through her into something beyond.

*"You seek purpose. You believe finding us will provide it. This is error. Purpose is not found. Purpose is built. We can give you power. Meaning, you must construct yourself."*

"Then why choose me?" Sera asked. "If I have to build my own meaning, what do you provide?"

*"Material,"* Wisdom said, and somehow the word was not insulting. *"We provide the material. You are the architect."*

The Third Head—War—was emerald, and it spoke in short, clipped sentences. *"You can fight. We've seen your memories. The Church trained you well. But fighting is not war. War is strategy. Patience. The willingness to lose battles to win the conflict. Can you be patient?"*

"I climbed a mountain for eleven years," Sera said. "I think I can be patient."

War's laugh was like swords being drawn from sheaths. *"Perhaps."*

The Fourth Head—Judgment—was gold, and its gaze made Sera want to confess every sin she'd ever committed. She resisted. Barely.

*"You are not worthy,"* Judgment said. *"Not yet. Perhaps not ever. Worthiness is not a state. It is a practice. You will be judged not by what you have done but by what you choose to do, moment by moment, for as long as the bond persists."*

"That's terrifying," Sera said.

*"Yes."* Judgment sounded pleased. *"Fear of judgment is the beginning of wisdom."*

The Fifth Head—Mercy—was white, and it wept as it spoke. Actual tears, pearlescent and warm, that fell on Sera's hands and healed small hurts she hadn't known she carried.

*"You blame yourself for surviving,"* Mercy said. *"This is false. Survival is not theft. Your father's sacrifice was his choice. Your mother's sacrifice was her choice. You did not steal their lives by keeping yours."*

Sera felt tears on her own face. "I know. I know that. I just... I can't feel it."

*"Then feel this,"* Mercy said, and pressed its forehead to hers.

The grief that hit Sera was not her own. It was older, vaster, the accumulated sorrow of a being who had watched civilizations rise and fall, who had loved mortals and lost them, who had made mistakes that could not be undone. It was Bahamut's grief, offered in solidarity.

*"You are not alone in your pain,"* Mercy whispered. *"You have never been alone."*

The Sixth Head—Mystery—was violet, and it spoke in riddles that Sera wouldn't understand for years.

*"The path forward is the path behind. The answer you seek is the question you fear. The bond will break when it becomes unbreakable."*

"That's not helpful," Sera said.

*"It is not meant to be helpful. It is meant to be true."*

And finally, the Seventh Head—Sovereignty—silver and central and somehow more real than the others, spoke with a voice that was all seven harmonized.

*"Sera Vane. You have been chosen. The bond we offer is not servitude. We do not seek a servant, a slave, a worshipper. We seek a partner. An equal. One who can speak for us when we have forgotten how to speak for ourselves. One who can remind us what it means to be... not divine. Not eternal. Just present. Just here. Just now."*

The wyrmling—all seven heads as one—extended a wing. It was small, still, barely enough to cover Sera's body. But it was warm, and it was real, and it was offering.

*"Will you accept this bond? Knowing it may cost your life? Knowing it will certainly cost your innocence? Knowing that you will change, and we will change, and neither of us can predict what we will become?"*

Sera thought of her mother, dead for a book. She thought of her father, who had believed that protecting his family was worth any price. She thought of herself, twelve years old in a well, drinking from a bucket rope and choosing, again and again, to take the next breath.

"Yes," she said.

The wing wrapped around her. The seven voices spoke as one.

*"Then let it be so."*

Pain was not the right word for what came next. Pain was too small, too human. This was transformation. Sera felt the bond form like a silver thread connecting her heart to seven others, felt her consciousness expand to encompass perspectives that were not her own, felt the boundaries of her self become permeable, flexible, negotiable.

She screamed. Or maybe she sang. Or maybe she did both, and neither, and something else entirely.

When awareness returned, she was different. She knew it immediately. The warmth of the crater floor against her knees was sharper, more detailed. The silver light had colors in it she couldn't name. And in her mind, patient and vast and newly intimate, were seven consciousnesses waiting to be known.

*"Rest now,"* Sovereignty said. *"The bond is formed. The journey begins."*

*"But first,"* Wrath added, *"you must learn to fly."*

---

### Part III: The Ascent

They emerged from the crater on the fourth day.

Bahamut was no longer wyrmling-sized. The bonding had accelerated his growth—he was now the size of a horse, maybe larger, his seven heads weaving independently as he tested the air with senses Sera was only beginning to understand.

She rode on his back. There was no saddle, no reins. There didn't need to be. She could feel his intentions as clearly as her own, and he could feel hers. When she wanted to go left, he knew. When he needed her to hold tighter, she felt it before he moved.

The bond. It was terrifying and exquisite and wrong in all the ways that growth is wrong—uncomfortable, stretching, necessary.

*"Are you ready?"* Seven voices, layered like chords.

Sera looked down. The crater floor was far below. She hadn't remembered him climbing. But then, she hadn't been paying attention to the physical world. She'd been learning the geography of her own mind, which now contained mountains she hadn't put there.

"No," she said. "But that's never stopped me before."

*"Good answer."*

The wings spread—silver membranes stretched between fingers of bone, catching light and transforming it into motion. Sera felt the moment before takeoff, the coiling of impossible muscles, the gathering of will against gravity.

Then they were rising.

The ascent was not smooth. Bahamut had not flown in centuries, and his bodies—seven brains, seven necks, seven sets of instincts—argued about technique. Wrath wanted power, brute force against the air. Wisdom wanted precision, each wingbeat calculated. War wanted speed, height gained quickly for tactical advantage.

Sera felt them arguing and, without thinking, intervened.

"You're fighting yourselves," she said aloud, her voice snatched by wind. "Let me."

She didn't know what she was doing. She didn't have the words for it. But she reached into the bond—that silver thread connecting her to seven hearts—and pulled. Not hard. Gently. Like adjusting a tuning string.

Harmony.

The seven heads stopped arguing. For one perfect moment, they moved as one, and the flight stabilized. They weren't rising anymore—they were *soaring*, catching an updraft that shouldn't have existed, spiraling upward on thermals that tasted of magic and possibility.

*"Yes,"* Sovereignty breathed, and the wonder in that single word was worth every step of the eleven-year climb.

They broke through the cloud layer.

The world spread below them like a map. Sera could see the Ashen Reaches, the devastation of the War of Shattered Wings still visible in the landscape's scarring. She could see the borders of kingdoms she'd only read about. She could see, impossibly far, the glint of the sea.

And above them: sky. Endless, open, available.

Sera laughed. She couldn't help it. The sound tore from her throat, wild and uncontrolled, nothing like the composed paladin she'd trained to be. This was the laugh of the girl in the well, the girl who had kept drinking from the bucket rope, the girl who had refused to die because dying would mean stopping.

*"You are laughing,"* Mercy observed, delighted.

"I'm flying!"

*"You are crying,"* Wisdom noted, clinically.

"I'm flying!"

*"You are afraid,"* Wrath said, approving.

"I'm flying!"

They circled Throne's Rest once, twice, a silver comet describing arcs around the mountain's peak. Sera felt the wind in her hair, the scales warm beneath her, the sevenfold consciousness surrounding her like a fortress.

She was not alone. She would never be alone again.

The thought should have terrified her. Instead, it felt like the first true thing she'd ever known.

"Where do we go?" she asked.

*"Wherever you choose,"* Sovereignty said. *"You are the rider. We are the wings. The direction is yours."*

Sera looked at the world spread before her. She thought of the Church that had housed her but never understood her. She thought of Vorgathrax, the dragon who had killed her family, somewhere out there preparing for war. She thought of herself, twelve years old, making a promise to survive.

"Forward," she said. "We go forward."

Bahamut's seven heads roared in harmony—a sound that shook the clouds and announced to any who could hear that the Seven-Headed Sovereign had awakened, that the Seventh Bond had been forged, that the world had changed while it wasn't looking.

They flew toward the sun.

And for the first time since she was twelve years old, Sera Vane did not look back.

## Author's Notes

### What This Scene Accomplishes

1. **Emotional Catharsis**: Sera's arc requires her to accept that survival is not guilt. This scene gives her that acceptance from a divine source—Mercy's forgiveness is external validation she can eventually internalize.

2. **Cosmic Establishment**: We see the Hoard-Heart, the seven eggs, the nature of the bond. The reader understands what Sera has agreed to.

3. **Voice Introduction**: Each of Bahamut's heads speaks with distinct personality. Wrath is terse and challenging. Wisdom is clinical. Mercy is emotional. This sets up their ongoing dynamic.

4. **Power Scaling**: Bahamut starts small (wyrmling-sized) and grows during the bonding. This gives us somewhere to go—he's not at full power yet, which creates tension for the coming conflict.

### Mechanical References

- The bond's telepathic nature is established (shared senses, shared intentions)
- Sera's ability to harmonize the heads foreshadows her ultimate role (teaching Sovereignty)
- The three-day duration references the traditional "wizard's familiar" bonding ritual, elevated to cosmic scale

### Scene Connections

- **Backward**: References Sera's childhood trauma (the well, the bucket rope) established in her character document
- **Forward**: Sets up the "flying lessons" sequence where Sera and Bahamut learn to fight together
- **Thematic**: The "climbing" metaphor runs throughout—Sera keeps climbing, keeps ascending, refuses to look back

### Tone Notes

This scene balances wonder with terror. The bonding is beautiful but painful. The flight is exhilarating but dangerous. Sera's emotional state is complex—she's not simply happy to be chosen, she's overwhelmed, uncertain, but ultimately willing.

That willingness is the key. She doesn't know what she's doing. She just knows she's not stopping.
